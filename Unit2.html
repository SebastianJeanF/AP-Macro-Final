Unit 2: Economic Indicators and the Business Cycle

Circular Flow and GDP

Product Market: where economic goods are services are exchanged
Resource Market where factors of production (resources [look at unit 1]) are exchanged
GDP: the total value of goods and services made in a year that were purchased in that same year
Components of GDP
  1. Consumer Spending: value of goods/services bought by consumers
  2. Investment Spending: spending done by business (to invest in themselves)
  3. Government Spending: spending done by the govt.
  4. Net Exports: Total imports subtracted from total exports

Limitations of GDP

GDP alone can't be used to measure the strength of the economy of a nation
  1. Population: Countries with the same GDP but different population sizes can't be compared
  2. Inequality: GDP doesn't tell how the money with different nations are distributed to the people (ex. equality, 50% cash owned by 1%)
  3. Environment: Environmental situations also dictate health of the economy
  4. Shadow Economy: Some transactions are not reported in the GDP measure

Unemployment

  • Labor Force: anyone 16 years or older willing and able to work (in the past 30 days)
  • Unemployment Rate: the percentage of those in the labor force who don't have a job
  • Labor Force Participation Rate: workers employed divided by the labor force
  • Natural Rate of Unemployment: rate of unemployment at full-employment level
  • Frictional Unemployment: unemployment caused by workers who are temporarily unemployed or currently in between jobs (ex. seasonal, waiting for offer)
  • Structural Unemployment: unemployment caused by workers whose skills have become obsolete (ex. technology)
  • Cyclical Unemployment: workers who are unemployed due to economic contraction; current U.E.R. - natural U.E.R.

Price Indices and Inflation

  • Consumer Price Index: a measure that evaluates how much prices have changes from base year
  • Inflation Rate: the percent change in the price level of the economy from year to year
  • Deflation vs Disinflation: the former is negative inflation, the latter is a lower inflation rate than the year prior
  • Real variables: variables where the effects of inflation have been factored out

Costs of Inflation

Unanticipated inflation can lead to unexpected effects, such as the following:
  • Menu Costs: firms changing prices on "menus" p(ex. Walmart hiring more employees to changing price tags frequently)
  • Shoe Leather Costs: the cost of additional time/effort people spend due to inflation (ex. businesses running to banks to get cash money more frequently)
  • Loss of Purchasing Power: the unit of cash in a nation being worth less
  • Wealth Redistribution: Real value of wealth being transferred from borrowers to lenders (due to unexpected changes in real interest rates for loans)
Inflation Rate is calculated by doing the following:
(Current Market Basket - Base Year Market Basket)/(Base Year Market Basket) x 100:

Real vs Nominal GDP

Nominal GDP: GDP that doesn't account for inflation (just the total dollar value)
Real GDP: nominal GDP divided by the GDP Deflator; more accurate since inflation is account for
GDP Deflator is calculated by the following:
(Nominal GDP) / (Real GDP) x 100

Business Cycles

RGDP above the growth trend line correlates with lower unemployment rate and higher aggregate price levels (the opposite is true)
Peak: the highest point of RGDP before a contraction starts
Trough: the lowest point of RGDP before a expansion starts
Recession: two straight fiscal quarters of RGDP contraction
Expansion: any period of RGDP growth
Contraction: any period of RGDP reduction
The Growth Trend Line: represents natural rate of RGDP growth, full employment. and the natural rate of unemployment
