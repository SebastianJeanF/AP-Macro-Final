Unit 4: Financial Sector

Financial Assets

  • Liquidity: the ease at which financial assets can be used to purchase things (turn to cash)
  • Rate of Return: net gain/loss of an investment over a period of time
  • Bond: interest-bearing assets issued by businesses or the government
  • Stock: a security that gives someone ownership in a company

Nominal vs. Real Interest rates

The formula for real interest rate is:
N.I.R. - Inflation = R.I.R.

Money

There are different categories of the money supply; in AP Macro, we use mainly M1 and M2 money
Money Supply What is included? Liquidity Level Function
M0 Monetary base (currency in circulation, bank reserves High Medium of Exchange
M1 Coins, paper currency, checkable deposits High Medium of Exchange
M2 Money accounts below $100K saving deposits, certificate of deposits, bonds and securities Medium Store of Value
M3 Money accounts above $100K, long-lasting certificate of deposits Low Unit of Account

Banking/Expansion of the Money Supply

In the United States banking system, we use Fractional Reserve Banking, where banks are required to hold a fraction of its deposits in reserves (required reserves)
Demand Deposit Reserve Ratio Required Reserves Excess Reserves
$5000 .25 $1250 $3750
$10,000 .2 $2,000 $8,000
Money Multiplier: how much the money supply increases based on a change in deposits; calculated by taking the inverse of the reserve ratio
Assets: the possessions that are owned by or credited to a bank and can be liquefied into cash (ex. required/excess reserves)
Liabilities: the financial obligations that a bank must pay to a customer, and they must be repaid when requested (ex. demand deposits)

Monetary Policy

The Federal Reserve has multiple ways that they can manipulate the money supply; they do this in order to ultimately influence the economy
Larger money supply -> lower interest rate -> more investment spending -> higher RGDP!!!
Monetary Tool for Money Supply Increase Money Supply Decrease Money Supply
Discount Rate Decrease the Discount Rate Raise the Discount Rate
Federal Funds Rate Decrease Federal Funds Rate Increase Federal Funds Rate
Open Market Operations Buy Bonds Sell Bonds
Reserve Ratio Decrease the Reserve Ratio Increase the Reserve Ratio
Actions that increase the money supply are called expansionary monetary policies, and vice-versa are called recessionary monetary policies
